interpretation and reflection :thoughtful: 
1. Why was it necessary to scale the features before performing hierarchical clustering?
    > Hierarchical clustering relies on Euclidean distance to measure the 
    similarity between data points. In our dataset, occurrence_no was a count 
    that could reach into the hundreds, while our age features (max_ma, min_ma)
     were much smaller numbers. Scaling the data using StandardScaler ensured 
     that features with larger numeric ranges did not unfairly dominate the 
     distance calculations, allowing the algorithm to weight the temporal range
      and occurrence counts equally.

2. What linkage method was used to build the dendrogram, and how does it work?
    > The project required using Complete Linkage. This method defines the 
    distance between two clusters as the maximum distance between their most 
    dissimilar members. This approach tends to produce highly compact, tightly
     bound clusters, which was useful for grouping dinosaur genera with very 
     similar temporal profiles and abundance.

3. How were the features for each genus engineered from the raw dataset?
    > The raw occurrence data was filtered for the 'genus' rank and then 
    aggregated. We used Pandas to find the oldest fossil age (max_ma) and
     youngest fossil age (min_ma) for each genus to determine its first and 
     last appearance. We also counted the total occurrences and derived a new
      temporal_range feature by subtracting the youngest age from the oldest,
       giving us a complete profile of that genus's evolutionary lifespan.

4. What does the resulting dendrogram reveal about the dinosaur genera?
    > The heavy clustering at the bottom of the un-truncated dendrogram (the 
    "barcode" effect) visually demonstrates the massive diversity of the 
    Dinosauria clade during the Mesozoic era. Because many branches merge at 
    very low distance thresholds, it indicates that numerous distinct genera 
    shared highly overlapping survival timelines and fossil abundance levels.
