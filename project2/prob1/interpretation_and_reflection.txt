1. how much variance was captured by the first 3 principal components?
    > Each individual principal component captures roughly 0.16666667 
    or 16.67% of the variance. Adding all these numbers together we get
    0.5, meaning that these components captured 50% of the total variance.

2. did the PCA project appear to separate the data clearly?
    > Based on the result of the scatter plot, the colored dots or data
    points were stacked in a very rigid way, almost perfectly straight
    in both vertical and horizontal columns. This means that the clusters
    overlap heavily within the "gridlines". Therefore, the PCA projection
    did not properly separate the data clearly into distinct, isolated 
    groups.

3. which value of k seemed best, and why?
    > After applying the elbow method, we could see in the plot that 
    k = 6 was a great option since the steep drop-off started to flatten
    out at k = 6.

4. were the clusters easy to interpret in terms of the original car attributes?
    > No. PCA takes the car attributes and mathematically mashes them
    into abstract principal components. The result, a bunch of colored 
    clusters in a graph, which don't make any sense when attempting to
    interpret them in terms of the original car attributes.

5. what limitations should we keep in mind when using k-means on encoded categorical data?
    > K-means relies on numeric distances... Having a categorical dataset
    made the processing more complicated. The original dataset had to be
    transformed into numerical values whose distances are not an accurate
    representation of reality. This caused the data to be structured 
    rigidly instead, making K-Means struggle to draw logical boundaries.
